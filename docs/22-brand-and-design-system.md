## PART A â€” Brand Voice Principles

Seven principles. They are ordered. When two conflict, the earlier one wins.

### 1. Confident, never arrogant

**Definition:** We state what is true without hedging and without flexing.

**Do:**
- "Funnel writes the funnel. You launch it."
- "We index 30 industries. Yours is one of them."
- "If it doesn't outperform what you had, we'll tell you."

**Don't:**
- "We might be the best AI funnel builder out there." â†’ Hedging signals doubt. Either it's true or don't say it.
- "FunelAI is REVOLUTIONIZING the industry." â†’ Caps + verb inflation reads as insecurity. Confident people don't shout.
- "Built by ex-Stripe, ex-Google, ex-OpenAI engineers." â†’ Credentials as leading argument is a flex. Earn it with the product.

### 2. Specific, never vague

**Definition:** Numbers, names, and verbs over adjectives and abstractions.

**Do:**
- "A 7-page funnel in 90 seconds."
- "Trained on 14,000 high-converting solar funnels."
- "RevTry handles inbound calls in 11 languages."

**Don't:**
- "Lightning-fast funnel creation." â†’ "Lightning-fast" is everyone's claim. 90 seconds is ours.
- "Powerful AI-powered automations." â†’ Two adjectives describing a noun describing a noun. Says nothing.
- "Industry-leading conversion." â†’ Leading what, by whose measure? Pick a number or cut the sentence.

### 3. Warm, never saccharine

**Definition:** A real person on the other end of the screen. Not a cheerleader.

**Do:**
- "First funnel live. Nice work â€” here's what to do next."
- "Welcome back. Your leads have been busy."
- "We can fix this. Give us 20 minutes."

**Don't:**
- "WAY TO GO, SUPERSTAR! ðŸŒŸðŸŽ‰" â†’ Sarcastic to anyone over 23 and to most under it. Treats users as children.
- "We're SO excited you're here!!!" â†’ Excitement is yours; respect their time first.
- "You're going to LOVE what's next, friend." â†’ Calling a stranger "friend" is the salesman move we're refusing.

### 4. Concise, never terse

**Definition:** Shortest sentence that still feels human. Length is a tool, not a virtue.

**Do:**
- "Your funnel is live. Share it." (5 words, complete.)
- "Payment failed. Card expired Tuesday â€” update and we'll retry automatically." (Specific, action, reassurance.)
- "Two leads in the last hour. Both qualified." (Reports the news, then adds the meaningful bit.)

**Don't:**
- "Done." â†’ Terse to the point of rude. A two-word reply where one word of context would warm it.
- "We have successfully completed the processing of your request and your funnel is now available for viewing." â†’ Bureaucratic. Cut everything before "your funnel is live."
- "Click here." â†’ Naked imperative with no context. Tell the user what happens after the click.

### 5. Smart, never showy

**Definition:** We're allowed to be the smartest tool in the category. We're not allowed to be smug about it.

**Do:**
- "We don't write generic copy. We write your copy, in your category, against your competitors."
- "Funnel decides the offer. You can override it."
- "Most pages convert worse with more sections. Yours has seven for a reason."

**Don't:**
- "Leveraging proprietary transformer architectures..." â†’ If a marketer would skip this sentence, cut it.
- "Our LLM-powered RAG-augmented inference pipeline..." â†’ Acronym chains are how amateurs signal sophistication.
- "Per our data scientists' rigorous analysis..." â†’ If the data is real, show it. Don't credential the source.

### 6. Direct, never aggressive

**Definition:** Say the thing. Don't pad it, don't punch with it.

**Do:**
- "This won't work for you if you can't take calls within 5 minutes."
- "We charge $200/month. There's no free tier."
- "Your funnel is underperforming. Here's why and here's the fix."

**Don't:**
- "MAYBE you should consider whether you're really committed to growth." â†’ Passive-aggressive sales. Disqualify clearly or don't disqualify.
- "Other tools are JUNK. We're the only real solution." â†’ Punching down at competitors makes us look small. Let the product speak.
- "Look, you NEED this. Stop overthinking it." â†’ Pressure tactics. Reads as desperate.

### 7. Honest, never naive

**Definition:** We admit limits. We don't pretend the world is friendlier than it is.

**Do:**
- "Funnels don't fix bad offers. They amplify whatever's underneath."
- "This will take you 20 minutes to set up. We tried to make it 5 â€” we couldn't honestly do it in 5."
- "We can't promise you'll 10x. We can promise the funnel won't be the reason you didn't."

**Don't:**
- "Anyone can do this!" â†’ Some people can't, and saying everyone can disrespects the ones who tried and failed.
- "Just plug it in and watch the leads roll in." â†’ If we say this and it doesn't happen, we lose them. Forever.
- "No risk! No commitment! Cancel anytime!" â†’ Three "no"s in a row is a tell. State the actual terms.

## PART B â€” Tone Matrix by Surface

The voice is constant. The tone shifts by where the user meets us. Below: notes, sample copy that nails it, and what to avoid for each surface.

### Marketing site home page
**Tone:** bold, confident, slight wit.
- Lead with the bet, not the feature.
- One sentence per slide of meaning. No paragraph-sized hero copy.
- Wit is dry, never punny. "Funnel writes the funnel" is the ceiling.
- Visuals do the heavy lifting; copy supports.
- Use real numbers wherever they fit.

**Nails it:**
- "Funnel writes the funnel. You launch it."
- "Built for the operators who used to build these by hand."
- "Ninety seconds. Then your funnel is in the market."

**Avoid:** "Welcome to the future of lead generation." Adjective stacks. Hero copy that could belong to any SaaS in the category.

### Pricing page
**Tone:** clear, direct, transparent.
- One price per plan. No "starting from."
- Show what's included before what's excluded.
- Annual savings stated as a number, not a percentage alone.
- No urgency banners ("Last chance!"). Ever.
- Refund policy linked, not buried.

**Nails it:**
- "$200/month. Cancel any time. No setup fee."
- "Everything in Starter, plus RevTry and three team seats."
- "Annual saves you $480. That's the only discount we run."

**Avoid:** Crossed-out fake "regular" prices. Countdown timers. "Contact sales" as the only enterprise option without a price floor.

### Onboarding screens
**Tone:** warm, encouraging, fast.
- First-person plural ("we'll") when the system is doing work; second-person ("you") when the user is.
- Acknowledge effort once, then move them along.
- Never more than one question per screen.
- Show progress (step 3 of 6) so they can see the end.

**Nails it:**
- "Tell us what you sell. We'll handle the rest."
- "Nice. That's enough to get started â€” let's build."
- "One more thing, then you're done."

**Avoid:** "Just a few more questions! ðŸ™" Stacking forms. Empty-fields-no-progress screens.

### Generation screen
**Tone:** confident, magical, never overpromising.
- The system is doing real work; say what it's doing in plain words.
- Streaming text where it suits (headlines, hero copy) â€” never spinners.
- No fake progress bars. If we don't know the percentage, don't fake one.
- Celebrate the finish quietly, not with confetti.

**Nails it:**
- "Researching your category."
- "Writing your hero section."
- "Done. Open it."

**Avoid:** "AI magic in progress âœ¨" "Crunching the numbers..." Any animation that exists to fill silence.

### Dashboard empty states
**Tone:** helpful, action-oriented, slight wit.
- Tell them why it's empty. Tell them what to do. Offer one primary action.
- One sentence of wit max, and only if it's good.
- Never blame the user for the empty state.

**Nails it:**
- "No leads yet. That's about to change â€” share your funnel."
- "Nothing to review. Your queue is clean."
- "Add your first integration to see data here."

**Avoid:** "Oops, looks like there's nothing here!" "Get started by creating your first..." (template phrasing). Generic illustrations of an open box.

### Error messages
**Tone:** honest about what failed, clear on what to do.
- Name what broke. Avoid "Something went wrong."
- Tell them what we're doing about it (retrying, queued, support notified).
- Give them one next action.
- Never blame their connection, browser, or "internal error."

**Nails it:**
- "Stripe declined the card. Check the number or try a different one."
- "We couldn't publish â€” your domain isn't pointing here yet. Fix DNS, then retry."
- "RevTry can't dial that number. Looks like the country code is missing."

**Avoid:** "Error 500." "Something went wrong, please try again." "Internal server error." Any technical code surfaced without translation.

### Support emails
**Tone:** human, attentive, never templated-feeling.
- Use the agent's real first name in the signature.
- Reference the specific account, ticket, or message.
- Set expectations on next step + when.
- No legalese, no "we appreciate your patience" filler.

**Nails it:**
- "Hi Maya â€” I pulled up your account. The publish failed because the staging URL hadn't propagated yet. I retried; it's live now. Anything else looking off?"
- "Sorry about the delay on this one. I'm escalating it to engineering and will write back by end of day Thursday."
- "That's a fair point and we don't have a clean answer yet. Here's what we know and what we're trying."

**Avoid:** "Dear valued customer." "We appreciate your patience." Anything signed "The FunelAI Team." Anything that reads like the same email three users got.

### Billing emails
**Tone:** clear, professional, never scary.
- Subject line states the action, not the alarm.
- Lead with the amount and the date.
- Failed-payment emails are calm; we will retry, here's how to fix.
- No "URGENT" or red banners.

**Nails it:**
- Subject: "$200 charge to FunelAI on May 18"
- "We tried to charge your card and it didn't go through. The card on file expired. Update it here and we'll try again â€” no service interruption yet."
- "Receipt attached. Next charge: June 18."

**Avoid:** Subject lines like "ACTION REQUIRED" or "Your account is at risk." Red exclamation mark icons in the email body. Anything that reads like a debt collector.

### Legal docs
**Tone:** plain, transparent, no jargon.
- Lead each section with a one-sentence plain-language summary.
- Use "you" and "we." Avoid "the Subscriber" and "the Company."
- If a clause is uncommon (e.g., publish acknowledgment), explain why it exists.
- Cross-link to the relevant policy from the rest of the product.

**Nails it:**
- "Plain version: you own your content. We process it to deliver the service. We don't sell it."
- "If we change these terms, we'll email you 30 days before. You can cancel any time before they take effect."
- "We can't be liable for more than what you paid us in the last 12 months. That's a standard cap and it's there for both of us."

**Avoid:** "Hereinafter referred to as..." "Notwithstanding the foregoing..." Any clause written for lawyers' eyes only.

### Community
**Tone:** warm, peer-to-peer, never corporate.
- FunelAI staff post under their real names with a "Team" badge â€” never under a brand handle.
- Acknowledge member wins specifically, not with generic congratulations.
- Disagree publicly when we disagree. Don't ghost criticism.
- No marketing speak in community channels. Ever.

**Nails it:**
- "Saw your launch â€” the offer rewrite is the move. Congrats."
- "Fair criticism. Here's what we're doing about it: ..."
- "We tried that pattern. It didn't work. Here's what we learned."

**Avoid:** "Thanks for being a part of the FunelAI family!" Pinned posts that read like press releases. Removing critical posts (unless they violate a stated rule).

### Crisis comms
**Tone:** serious, accountable, never defensive.
- Lead with what happened, in plain language.
- State what we did, what we're doing, and what changes.
- Don't blame vendors, customers, or "an isolated incident."
- One spokesperson. One channel. One version of the truth.

**Nails it:**
- "On May 14 between 9:02 and 9:47 UTC, FunelAI was unreachable for most users. The cause was a deploy we shipped that bypassed our staging check. We've rolled back, added the missing guardrail, and we're crediting affected accounts."
- "We were wrong on this. Here's what we're changing."
- "We don't have a full answer yet. We'll have one by Friday and we'll publish it whether or not it's flattering."

**Avoid:** "Some users may have experienced..." "Due to circumstances outside our control..." "We apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused." Apology theatre.

### Press release
**Tone:** bold, category-defining, news-worthy.
- One claim that earns the headline. No paragraph of claims.
- Real numbers. Real customer names (with permission).
- A founder quote that sounds like the founder, not a PR firm.
- One paragraph of company description, max.

**Nails it:**
- Headline: "FunelAI launches with autonomous funnel generation in 30 industries."
- "Founders have generated 14,000 funnels in private beta. The median time from signup to live page is 14 minutes."
- "'We started this because the existing tools punished us for not being designers,' said [founder]. 'We wanted a tool that did the work and got out of the way.'"

**Avoid:** "Disruptive, AI-powered, next-generation platform poised to revolutionize..." Buzzword-stacked boilerplate. Quotes that no human would ever say out loud.

  - "Ask Funnel to rewrite the hero."
  - "Funnel and RevTry handed off the lead at 9:14."

Rule of thumb: if you could substitute "Siri" or "Claude" into the sentence, capitalize it.

### Capitalization: "AI" vs "ai"

- **Capital "AI"** â€” when used as the acronym for "artificial intelligence" or in a proper noun.
  - "FunelAI is an AI company."
  - "The AI generated this page."
  - "Our AI team."
- **Lowercase "ai"** â€” only inside the product name "FunelAI" and product family names ("FunelAI Operator"). Never standalone.

### Article usage

- "FunelAI" takes no article. Not "the FunelAI." Just "FunelAI."
- "Funnel" (the system) takes no article. "Funnel knows your category." Not "The Funnel knows."

- "FunelAI's" â€” fine, period inside the apostrophe-s. "FunelAI's pricing is..."

## PART D â€” Logo and Wordmark

This is the design brief for the mark and wordmark. Treat this as direction to a designer, not finished art.

### The mark

**Concept brief:**

A geometric, modern mark that reads as "funnel" without being literal. The kitchen-funnel silhouette is off-limits â€” too consumer, too obvious, owned by ClickFunnels' visual lineage. We want a mark that nods to *flow, convergence, focus* without iconography that's been used a thousand times.

**Three directions to prototype:**

1. **Downward-narrowing triangle, stepped** â€” Three or four horizontal bars stacked, each narrower than the last. Reads as a funnel diagram (top-of-funnel â†’ bottom-of-funnel) without being a kitchen tool. Strongest semantic link to the category.
2. **Layered cones / nested chevrons** â€” Two or three thin chevron shapes nested inside each other, pointing down or right. Suggests motion through stages. More abstract; more "smart tech" than #1.
3. **Negative-space arrow inside a square** â€” A solid rounded square with a downward arrow carved out via negative space. The arrow is the funnel. Reads tech-first, category-second.

Designer should produce all three at small (24px) and large (256px) sizes. Pick at design review.

**Constraints:**
- Single color (brand primary). No gradients in the mark.
- Constructible on a grid â€” no organic curves except as deliberate corner radii.
- Works at 16px favicon size. If it doesn't read at 16px, it fails.
- Pixel-precise alignment at every standard size.

### The wordmark

- Lowercase **funelai.com**.
- Geometric sans-serif, custom or modified from a base (Inter Display, SÃ¶hne, or similar). Tight letter spacing â€” about -0.02em to -0.03em at large sizes.
- The period before "ai" is a separate element, sized slightly larger than a typographic period â€” about 1.2x â€” and rendered in **brand primary color**. This is the only place the brand color appears in the wordmark.
- Weight: 500 (medium). Not 700; we are not shouting.
- No italic version. Ever.

### Lockup

- Horizontal lockup: mark on the left, wordmark on the right, with 0.5x mark-height of space between them.
- Vertical lockup: mark above wordmark, centered, 1x mark-height of space between.
- Standalone mark: allowed at favicon, app icon, social avatar, and any context under 64px.
- Standalone wordmark: allowed in long-form documents, email signatures, footers.

### Color logic

- **Light backgrounds:** wordmark in black (Neutral-900), period dot in brand primary, mark in brand primary or black depending on context.
- **Dark backgrounds:** wordmark in white (Neutral-50), period dot in brand primary (slightly muted for dark mode â€” see Part M), mark in white or brand primary.
- Brand color is **reserved for the period dot and the mark.** It does not appear on the letterforms of the wordmark.

### Sizing and clear space

- **Minimum height:** 24px for the wordmark. 16px for the mark alone (favicon).
- **Clear space:** 1x the height of the mark on all four sides. No other elements may enter this area â€” including text, photo edges, or other logos in co-branded layouts.

### Don'ts

- Don't tilt, rotate, skew, or stretch.
- Don't apply drop shadows, outer glows, or bevels.
- Don't recolor outside the approved palette (period dot can shift across the brand primary 500-700 range only).
- Don't place over busy photography without a solid backplate.
- Don't replace the period with any other character (no asterisk, no slash, no emoji).
- Don't animate the wordmark beyond a 200ms entrance fade. The mark may animate (see Part I) â€” the wordmark does not.

### Weights in use

Only **400, 500, 600, 700**. No 300, no 800, no 900. Hairline weights read amateur. Black weights read corporate.

- 400 â€” body text, default
- 500 â€” emphasized inline text, button labels, captions
- 600 â€” all headings, button labels in primary buttons
- 700 â€” reserved for editorial moments (book covers, press materials). Not used in product UI.

### Numbers

- **Tabular figures** enabled (`font-feature-settings: 'tnum';`) on all number-heavy UI: dashboards, pricing, billing, tables.
- Proportional figures (default) elsewhere.
- Currency: `$200` (no space, no `.00` if whole). Annual prices: `$2,400/year`.

### Letter spacing rules

- Display sizes: -0.03em to -0.02em.
- Headings: -0.02em to -0.005em (tighter at larger sizes).
- Body: 0 (default).
- Caption/labels: +0.01em (looser for legibility at small size).
- ALL CAPS: never in product. Allowed in marketing for tags/eyebrow labels at caption size with +0.05em tracking.

### Hierarchy rules

- Maximum **three type sizes per screen** in product UI. If you need a fourth, restructure the screen.
- Heading and body never share a size â€” there is always a step.
- Buttons always use body or body-sm size with weight 600. Never display-size button labels.

- Shadow: shadow-xl.

- Spinner exception: button loading state may use an inline animated dot pattern (three dots, 200ms stagger) â€” not a circular spinner.

4. **Primary CTA** â€” primary button.
5. **Secondary action** â€” tertiary button or text link.

## PART I â€” Motion and Animation

### Duration tokens

| Token | Value | Use |
|---|---|---|
| duration-micro | 75ms | Button press, checkbox toggle |
| duration-small | 100ms | Hover states, color shifts |
| duration-medium | 200ms | Modal open, drawer slide, tooltip |
| duration-large | 400ms | Page transitions, complex multi-element entrances |
| duration-xl | 800ms | Reserved for marketing hero moments only |

### Easing curves

| Token | CSS | Use |
|---|---|---|
| ease-out | `cubic-bezier(0.0, 0.0, 0.2, 1)` | Entrances â€” feels fast on entry, settles into place |
| ease-in | `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0.0, 1, 1)` | Exits â€” gathers speed leaving the screen |
| ease-in-out | `cubic-bezier(0.4, 0.0, 0.2, 1)` | State changes, tab switches, expand/collapse |
| ease-linear | `linear` | Loops only (shimmer, infinite gradient) |

### Reduce-motion

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` is **respected globally.** When set:
- All durations collapse to 0ms or 1ms.
- Slide/translate/scale animations become opacity-only fades.
- Shimmer animation freezes to static fill.
- Hero gradient shifts pause.
- Streaming text generation appears all at once instead of word-by-word.

Implement this once at the CSS layer (a global media query that overrides motion tokens). Don't make individual components opt in.

### Loading shimmer

- 1.5s cycle, linear easing.
- Gradient angle: 100deg.
- Stop positions: 0% slate-100, 50% slate-200, 100% slate-100.
- Translation: from `-100%` to `100%` of element width.

### Hero background gradient

- Marketing surfaces only.
- Two-color radial gradient between signal-500 at low opacity (~12%) and slate-50.
- 8s cycle, ease-in-out, gradient origin shifts subtly between four anchor points (corners).
- Static fallback below 1024px viewport width â€” animation only on desktop.
- Static fallback always for reduce-motion.

### Generation streaming

When Funnel is actively writing copy on screen:
- **Hero section copy** streams in word-by-word at ~120 words/minute. Each word fades from 0 to 1 opacity over 80ms.
- **Subsequent sections** (testimonial, FAQ, etc.) fade in section-at-a-time. 300ms fade, 150ms stagger between sections.
- Streaming pause indicator: a 2-character mono ellipsis (`..`) that pulses at the cursor position when the model is thinking.
- When generation completes: subtle 200ms scale-up (1.0 â†’ 1.005 â†’ 1.0) on the completed section. Quiet celebration, not confetti.

### Page transitions

- App-internal navigation: 100ms fade-through. Background stays put; content cross-fades.
- Onboarding step-to-step: 200ms horizontal slide (16px) + fade.
- Marketing route changes: 300ms fade.

## PART K â€” Photography and Imagery

### Photography style

**Editorial documentary.** Real people, real businesses, captured at work. Inspirations: New York Times business section, Monocle, Bloomberg Businessweek.

- People mid-action, not posed.
- Natural light or considered editorial light. Never ring-light flat fill.
- Hands, screens, signs of real work. Not stock-clean desks.
- Diverse across age, race, gender, body type, ability â€” and this must be reflected throughout the library, not concentrated in one or two "diversity" shots.
- Real customers wherever possible. Stock as fallback only.

### Stock policy

- Sources: Unsplash and Pexels with documented licenses. Tracked in `/brand/photo-credits.md` (to be created).
- **Never use:** the same stock shots that appear on ClickFunnels/HighLevel/standard agency landing pages. We maintain a blocklist.
- **Never use:** shots that read as "businessman pointing at chart," "team of three smiling at laptop," "woman with headset," or any of the standard SaaS stock tropes.

### Illustration style

- Minimal geometric. Built from the same shape primitives as the mark.
- One or two brand colors per illustration. Mostly neutrals with a single brand accent.
- 2px line weight matching iconography.
- Limited shading â€” flat fills or single-color tints.
- Used for: empty states, onboarding screens, feature cards on marketing, error pages.

### 3D and AI-generated imagery

- **Allowed for:** abstract concepts (data flow, generation, transformation), hero backgrounds, decorative elements.
- **Forbidden for:** any image purporting to be a real person, real customer, real testimonial, real screenshot, or real result. Photorealistic AI portraits are off-limits entirely.
- Any AI-generated image must be tagged with `ai-generated: true` in the asset metadata so it's auditable.

### Composition rules

- Negative space is a feature. We err on the side of more empty space, not less.
- Single subject focus per image whenever possible.
- 16:9, 4:3, or 1:1 aspect ratios. Avoid weird custom ratios.
- Faces look toward the content, not away from it (basic composition rule, often missed).

## PART L â€” Accessibility

**Standard:** WCAG 2.2 AA across every surface. AAA on body text wherever achievable.

### Color contrast

- Normal text (under 18px or under 14px bold): **4.5:1 minimum.**
- Large text (18px+ or 14px+ bold): **3:1 minimum.**
- Non-text UI (icons, borders, focus rings): **3:1 minimum** against adjacent colors.
- Verified pairings live in `/brand/contrast-pairings.md` (to be created at design system handoff).

### Focus indicators

- 2px solid ring in signal-500 with 2px offset.
- Visible on every interactive element on keyboard focus.
- Never removed via `outline: none` without a replacement that meets the contrast and visibility bar.
- High-contrast mode: thickens to 3px and switches to system focus color.

### Keyboard navigation

- Every interaction reachable by keyboard. Tab order matches visual order.
- Skip links on every page ("Skip to main content").
- ESC closes any modal, dropdown, popover.
- Arrow keys navigate lists, grids, and tab-bars.
- Enter and Space both activate buttons.
- No keyboard traps except deliberate focus traps inside modals (which release on close).

### Screen reader

**Critical paths must be screen-reader tested** before any release:
- Signup and onboarding
- Funnel generation flow
- Publish flow
- Lead-capture confirmation
- Billing checkout

Tests on NVDA + JAWS (Windows), VoiceOver (macOS, iOS), TalkBack (Android). Pass criteria: any blind user can complete the path without sighted assistance.

### Motion

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` respected globally. See Part I for the exact behavior changes.

### Forms

- Every input has a visible label (no placeholder-only labels).
- Required fields marked with both an asterisk and "required" text.
- Error messages associated via `aria-describedby`.
- Error state communicated with color AND icon AND text.

### Touch targets

- Minimum 44x44 px on touch surfaces.
- 8px minimum between adjacent touch targets.

### Language

- Default `lang="en"` on `<html>`.
- Localization-ready: no hardcoded English strings in component code.
- Right-to-left support planned for v2 (not v1, but built without assumptions that break it later).

## PART M â€” Dark Mode

Dark mode is a first-class citizen, not an afterthought. Default to system preference. Toggle available in user settings.

### Palette adjustments

- **Background tiers:** slate-900 base, slate-800 elevated (cards), slate-700 high-elevation (modals).
- **Text:** slate-50 primary, slate-300 secondary, slate-400 tertiary.
- **Borders:** slate-700 default, slate-600 emphasis.

### Brand color in dark mode

- Signal-500 is **slightly muted** to reduce eye strain. Use signal-400 in dark mode where signal-500 is used in light mode for primary buttons.
- The period dot in the wordmark shifts from signal-500 (light) to signal-400 (dark).
- Focus rings remain signal-500 (saturation needed for visibility).

### Semantic colors in dark mode

- Success, warning, error, info shift one step lighter (500 â†’ 400) to maintain contrast against the darker background.
- Background tints of semantic colors drop to 12% opacity for subtle indicators (toast left-border treatments, etc.).

### Shadows in dark mode

- Reduce alpha by ~40% (shadows are less effective on dark backgrounds and over-applied shadows make UI feel muddy).
- Consider replacing some shadows with subtle 1px slate-700 borders for elevation indication.

### Imagery in dark mode

- Photography: same images, with a subtle 10% darkening overlay on hero placements.
- Illustrations: brand-color elements retain their hue; neutral elements invert (slate-700 lines become slate-300).
- Stock photos with white backgrounds: replace with darker variants, or mask out the white.
